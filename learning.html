Playbooks

Turn education into action.

Simple owner and new-broker playbooks: what to confirm, what to collect, and where deals usually get delayed, discounted, or retraded.

Free

Prepare to Sell

Clean the story before buyers pick it apart.

  1. Update the rent roll with unit mix, lease dates, current rent, legal rent if applicable, deposits, arrears, and vacant units.
  2. Prepare a T12 or year-to-date income and expense statement with backup for taxes, insurance, utilities, repairs, payroll, and management.
  3. Confirm the legal use: certificate of occupancy, unit count, zoning, retail use, open permits, and violations.
  4. Separate current performance from upside. Buyers need to know what is real today versus what requires turnover, renovation, lease-up, or approvals.
  5. Identify the likely buyer pool: local operator, 1031 buyer, developer, end user, institutional buyer, or value-add investor.
  6. Build a defensible value range using NOI/cap rate, GRM, price/SF, price/unit, and relevant comps.
  7. Fix obvious information gaps before launch. Messy information creates discounts, delays, and retrades.
Growth

Refinance

Position the asset for stronger loan proceeds.

  1. Prepare a clean T12 or annual income and expense statement. A lender will need actual income and actual operating expenses, not just a rough estimate.
  2. Confirm current NOI using the rent roll, collections, taxes, insurance, utilities, repairs, payroll, management, and recurring operating costs.
  3. Prepare lender documents: current rent roll, leases, T12/YTD financials, tax bills, insurance, utility backup, ownership documents, entity documents, mortgage statement, and schedule of capital improvements.
  4. Estimate DSCR, debt yield, LTV, amortization, interest rate, and reserve requirements before assuming cash-out proceeds.
  5. Clarify the purpose of the refinance: maturity risk, better rate/terms, renovation proceeds, partner buyout, cash-out, or stabilizing the asset.
  6. Stress-test rates, vacancy, insurance, taxes, repairs, and upcoming CapEx so the owner understands what the loan still supports if conditions change.
  7. Compare refinance proceeds against the alternative: hold as-is, renovate first, bring rents/expenses in line, or sell.
Growth

Renovate / Improve

Spend only where it moves value.

  1. Identify the value lever before spending: higher rent, lower expense, lower vacancy, better financing, reduced risk, or stronger buyer confidence.
  2. Estimate total project cost, downtime, lost rent, permits, contractor risk, contingency, and expected rent lift before starting work.
  3. Prioritize items that protect value first: roof, façade, boiler/HVAC, plumbing, electric, life safety, leaks, violations, and common-area condition.
  4. For unit renovations, compare the realistic post-renovation rent against the cost and downtime. Do not assume the market will pay for overbuilt finishes.
  5. Track invoices, permits, warranties, photos, before/after condition, and unit-level costs so the improvement can be proven later.
  6. Update the valuation after the work is complete using the new rent roll, expense profile, and realistic stabilized NOI.
Free

Hold / Optimize

Improve operations before deciding to sell.

  1. Benchmark major expenses: taxes, insurance, water/sewer, heat, electric, repairs, payroll, management, and legal/accounting.
  2. Review rent roll against market rents, legal limits, lease expirations, vacancy, arrears, and tenant turnover risk.
  3. Fix recurring operational leaks: high water usage, uncontrolled utilities, repeat repairs, poor collections, vacant storage/parking, and weak management systems.
  4. Create a rolling 12-month view of income and expenses instead of relying on memory or rough annual numbers.
  5. Decide annually whether the asset is best held, refinanced, renovated, or sold based on NOI, market demand, debt conditions, and owner goals.
  6. Keep a clean digital property file so the owner can move quickly when a lender, buyer, or broker asks for information.
Growth

Buy / Underwrite

Separate real upside from broker math.

  1. Start with actual income, not pro forma. Confirm rent roll, leases, arrears, concessions, deposits, and vacant units.
  2. Normalize expenses realistically. Add management, repairs, vacancy, payroll, insurance, taxes, utilities, and reserves if they are missing or understated.
  3. Verify legal and physical risk: certificate of occupancy, DHCR history if applicable, violations, permits, zoning, environmental concerns, roof, façade, and building systems.
  4. Model debt using current rates, amortization, DSCR, debt yield, LTV, closing costs, reserves, and stress-tested exit assumptions.
  5. Require proof for every upside claim: market rent comps, renovation budget, lease-up timeline, zoning support, expense savings, or comparable sales.
  6. Price the risk before making the offer, not after signing contract.
Free

OM Prep

Make the asset easier to understand and trust.

  1. Gather property facts: address, block/lot, tax class, zoning, lot size, building size, unit mix, year built, square footage, and photos.
  2. Prepare the income package: current rent roll, lease expirations, commercial lease terms, other income, vacancy, arrears, and pro forma assumptions.
  3. Prepare the expense package: taxes, insurance, water/sewer, heat, electric, repairs, payroll, management, legal, accounting, and recent CapEx.
  4. Confirm core metrics: NOI, cap rate, GRM, price/SF, price/unit, expense ratio, occupancy, and upside value.
  5. Highlight location and demand drivers: transit, retail corridors, employment, schools, development activity, comparable sales, and buyer demand.
  6. Be clear about risk. Investors trust a clean explanation of issues more than vague hype.
